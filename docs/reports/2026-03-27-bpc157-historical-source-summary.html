Historical perspective

1. The story begins with healing and cytoprotection

The oldest indexed source currently in the repository is the 1997 paper PMID 9403790, which presents BPC-157 as a gastric pentadecapeptide associated with healing effects in rat models. That supports a narrow historical claim: BPC-157 has been part of the experimental literature since at least the 1990s, but that early literature is preclinical rather than clinical.

2. By the 2000s, the literature points to named development programs

The 2007 paper PMID 17713731 matters because it references inflammatory bowel disease trial identifiers such as PL-10, PLD-116, and PL14736. That is historically important, but this repository does not yet have a complete published outcome trail for those programs.

3. The 2010s expand the musculoskeletal and mechanistic literature

The current library now includes a larger musculoskeletal block, including PMID 20225319 on rat ligament healing, PMID 14554208 on Achilles tendon healing, PMID 16583442 on tendon-to-bone healing, and PMID 21030672 on tendon fibroblast migration and signaling. These help explain why BPC-157 later entered sports-medicine and recovery discussions.

4. In the 2020s, public enthusiasm exceeds the human evidence base

The most useful current synthesis in the repository is the 2025 systematic review PMID 40756949, which reports 36 included studies from 1993 to 2024, with 35 preclinical studies and 1 clinical study. That ratio is the cleanest single-frame summary of the current evidence base in this project.

The repository has since grown to 59 traceable sources, but the newer additions mostly deepen the preclinical map across liver injury, portal hypertension, hemostasis, pain, alcohol injury, nitric-oxide-system work, fistula healing, and multisystem cytoprotection. They make the library more complete without materially changing the human-evidence conclusion.

Source inventory with actual documents

Foundational historical and preclinical papers

PMID 8298609 anchors the earliest conceptual framing of BPC.

PMID 9403790 anchors the early healing literature. PMID 17713731 extends that history and ties the literature to named IBD development programs.

Preclinical musculoskeletal and mechanistic studies

PMID 14554208, PMID 16583442, PMID 20225319 and PMID 21030672 are central examples, while later additions such as PMID 34829776 and PMID 20190676 broaden the muscle and myotendinous side of the map. These are examples of the kind of work that drives downstream repair claims.

Gastrointestinal, liver, vascular, and hemostasis studies

The expanded library now includes PMID 29358856, PMID 11595456, PMID 32547687, PMID 32226643, and PMID 31976029. This cluster helps explain why BPC-157 is often presented as a broad cytoprotective or vascular-modulating peptide rather than a narrow repair compound.

Accessible human-facing records

NCT02637284 is the clearest formal human trial registry entry currently in the repo.

PMID 34324435 is a small retrospective knee-pain report, and PMID 40131143 is a two-person IV safety pilot. Together they show that human reporting exists, but remains too limited for strong clinical conclusions.

Evidence syntheses and orientation reviews

PMID 40005999 provides a literature-and-patent review, PMID 40756949 provides the most useful current systematic review, and PMID 41490200 shows how BPC-157 is framed in current orthopaedic peptide discussions.

Official regulatory and sports-governance sources

The repository includes USADA guidance and the FDA compounding safety-risk page. These are not efficacy studies, but they are essential for responsible public language.

What this source set supports

  • BPC-157 has a real indexed history in experimental research.
  • The current literature in this repo is dominated by preclinical animal, mechanistic, injury-model, and review work.
  • The accessible human source base remains small.
  • The strongest recent synthesis still shows a major human-evidence gap.
  • Official sources support caution around approval status, safety, and athlete use.

What it does not support

  • Strong efficacy claims in humans.
  • A fully reconstructed publication trail for the older IBD development program.
  • A settled safety profile for routine clinical use.
