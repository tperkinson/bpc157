# BPC-157: History and Evidence Snapshot

Date: 2026-03-27

## Bottom line

BPC-157 has a long preclinical literature and a short human literature. The library in this repository now tracks 59 traceable sources, but that larger source count should not be confused with stronger human proof. Most of the expansion comes from older and newer preclinical papers, reviews, and mechanistic or injury-model studies rather than new controlled human trials.

## History in brief

The indexed literature reaches back to the 1990s, with early wound-healing papers describing BPC-157 as a gastric pentadecapeptide with broad protective effects in experimental models. A large share of the older literature is connected to the same research network in Croatia, especially around Zagreb.

By 2007, papers were already referring to BPC-157 as having been used in inflammatory bowel disease trials under identifiers such as PL-10, PLD-116, and PL14736. That point still matters historically, and the expanded catalog adds more papers that reuse those identifiers or reinforce the same gastrointestinal and cytoprotection story. Even so, the modern public record still has a thin set of accessible, published human trial results.

## What counts as a real study here

For this project, real studies include:

- indexed journal articles in PubMed
- registered human trial records on ClinicalTrials.gov
- official FDA or USADA/WADA guidance when it changes the real-world interpretation of claims

The library deliberately separates these from podcasts, vendor pages, wellness-clinic marketing, and secondhand social media summaries.

## The evidence stack

### 1. Preclinical evidence is abundant

The expanded catalog shows that the preclinical literature is broader than the first pass suggested. There are now direct papers in the library covering:

- wound healing
- tendon and ligament repair
- tendon-to-bone and myotendinous junction injury
- gastrointestinal injury models
- liver injury and portal hypertension
- ischemia-reperfusion and vascular/hemodynamic models
- platelet aggregation, bleeding time, and thrombocytopenia
- pain, serotonin, amphetamine, diazepam, and neuroleptic-related models
- angiogenesis and nitric oxide signaling

Representative examples now include the 1993 foundational BPC review, the 1997 healing paper, the 2003 Achilles tendon paper, the 2010 rat ligament paper, the 2011 tendon fibroblast/mechanistic paper, the 2017 colitis paper, and later studies on fistula healing, liver ischemia-reperfusion, and hemostasis. These studies are useful for plausibility and for mapping the scope of the literature, but they are not substitutes for controlled human trials.

### 2. Human evidence is still sparse

The clearest human-facing items identified in this pass are:

- a registered phase I trial record in healthy volunteers (`NCT02637284`) with unknown status and no posted results
- a small 2021 retrospective knee-pain study with 17 charts and non-standardized follow-up
- a 2025 two-person intravenous safety pilot reporting no adverse effects in those two participants

This is still too little to justify confident clinical claims. The catalog is much larger now, but the human side of the evidence stack is still narrow.

### 3. The catalog got broader, but the gap did not close

The 2025 HSS Journal systematic review is still the cleanest recent synthesis for musculoskeletal use. It reported 36 included studies from 1993 to 2024:

- 35 preclinical studies
- 1 clinical study

That ratio still captures the core state of the evidence base. The repository now contains substantially more direct BPC-157 papers than the first pass, but the new additions mostly deepen the preclinical map rather than changing the human-evidence balance.

### 4. What changed after reference mining

After several rounds of mining the reference lists from review papers already in the library, the project now includes 59 traceable sources. The biggest changes are:

- a stronger historical base reaching back to foundational 1993 and 1997 papers
- a much broader inventory of musculoskeletal, gastrointestinal, vascular, liver, hemostasis, toxicity, pain, and neuropharmacology studies
- a clearer view that the literature is internally dense and often self-referential, with many papers connected to recurring author groups and recurring mechanism narratives

That makes the repository more complete as a map of the literature, but it does not materially change the conclusion about human evidence.

## Public-communication stance

The current public site and internal notes should describe BPC-157 this way:

- historically interesting
- preclinically active in many models
- weakly supported in humans
- not approved for routine clinical use
- risky to overstate because regulatory and anti-doping bodies still treat it as an unapproved substance
